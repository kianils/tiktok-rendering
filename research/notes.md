## Introduction

Scholarly conversations about social media frequently oscillate between euphoria and moral panic: platforms connect people; platforms ruin people. Both tones can obscure a more durable structural question—how commercial institutions convert everyday conduct into operational knowledge. This paper takes that structural question as its center of gravity. It uses Shoshana Zuboff’s account of surveillance capitalism to interpret short-form video environments, and it uses platform studies scholarship to avoid treating algorithms as either magical or trivial. The motivating case is TikTok-like feeds, not because they are uniquely evil, but because their interaction economics make the extraction story unusually clear: many gestures per minute, rapid experimentation, and a feedback-rich interface that learns quickly from micro-behavior.

When a person opens a short-form video application, the interaction feels small: a thumb flick, a pause, a half-smile, a search typed in a hurry. Cumulatively, however, those gestures become something else. They are translated—often invisibly—into behavioral data that platforms can sort, score, and feed back into systems designed to shape future attention. Shoshana Zuboff’s account of *surveillance capitalism* gives language for that translation: lived experience is continuously *rendered* into operational facts, and much of what is extracted functions as *behavioral surplus*, fuel for prediction and optimization beyond what any narrow “service moment” strictly requires (Zuboff 8–11). This paper argues that short-form video environments such as TikTok intensify that dynamic because they produce dense, high-frequency traces that are especially legible to machine learning pipelines, while users rarely receive symmetric visibility into how those traces become models that act upon them. The argument proceeds in three steps. First, it clarifies Zuboff’s concepts of rendering and surplus and connects them to platform governance scholarship. Second, it explains why short-form interfaces are structurally favorable to rapid surplus generation. Third, it discusses how official data portability exports can function as a limited but politically meaningful site of *counter-rendering*: not a perfect mirror of internal systems, but a partial evidence trail that can make the conversion from life to data less opaque—provided interpreters avoid overclaiming.

The paper’s normative upshot is cautious. Data downloads do not “expose the algorithm” in a literal engineering sense; they expose *what the platform is willing to disclose* about user-associated records, which is incomplete by design and by regulatory circumstance. Even so, portability can support democratic and pedagogical goals: it can help users see *that* rendering happens, *what categories* of life are being operationalized, and *where* mitigation might interrupt dense feedback loops.

## Surveillance Capitalism as a Theory of Rendering and Surplus

Zuboff’s work is often summarized as a critique of corporate surveillance. That summary is true but too thin for the purposes of this paper. Her more distinctive contribution is structural: she describes an economic logic in which companies claim human experience as a free raw material—*behavioral surplus*—that is refined into *prediction products* sold in *behavioral futures markets* (Zuboff 8–11). The key move is not merely “collection,” but *rendering*: the process through which heterogeneous human conduct is converted into standardized signals that can travel through computational supply chains (Zuboff 95–100). Once rendered, behavior becomes comparable, clusterable, and actionable in ways that exceed the user’s situational understanding.

Two clarifications prevent misunderstanding. First, “surplus” in Zuboff’s sense is not a moral judgment that every datum is *unnecessary* in a strict engineering audit; it is a political-economic claim about scale and purpose. Even data that helps a service function can become surplus when it is stockpiled, combined, and repurposed for adjacent optimization projects that serve external clients or internal growth metrics (Zuboff 76–82). Second, “prediction products” need not mean only advertising. Prediction is a flexible commodity: it can inform ranking, retention, content experimentation, risk scoring, and the design of interfaces that nudge conduct toward measurable engagement (Zuboff 11–15). The breadth matters analytically because it connects the feed’s intimate feel to a wider machinery of behavioral governance.

This framework helps clarify why “personalization” can be simultaneously experienced as convenience and as power asymmetry. Personalization is not only a menu of tailored content; it is also evidence of a parallel process in which the user is reconstructed as a knowable object for someone else’s optimization problem. Zuboff’s language is helpful because it refuses a purely technological description. The issue is not only “data,” but *authority over the meaning of behavior*—who gets to define what a pause “means,” what a binge “predicts,” and what kinds of futures are financially valuable.

Nick Couldry and Ulises A. Mejias extend and reframe related concerns through the metaphor of *data colonialism*. They argue that appropriating human life as data is not a neutral infrastructural shift but a social relation: it extracts value through classification and connection-making that reorganizes everyday practices toward capitalizable outputs (Couldry and Mejias 18–22). Their account dovetails with Zuboff’s in insisting that the political stakes involve *social ordering*, not only privacy in the narrow sense of secrecy. Even when data is “about me,” its primary economic function may be *for others*—advertisers, risk scorers, retention systems—rather than for my own autonomous projects.

For platform studies, Tarleton Gillespie’s analysis of platforms as *custodial* institutions is also illuminating. Platforms present themselves as open surfaces for expression, but they continuously adjudicate visibility, rank content, and manage ambiguity through moderation and ranking systems whose details are partially hidden (Gillespie 1–5). The custodial frame matters for Zuboffian analysis because rendering is not only a market process; it is also a *governance* process. Ranking is a way of deciding what counts as relevant, what counts as safe, and what counts as “engaging.” Users experience outcomes; they rarely inspect the full decision stack.

Gillespie’s work is particularly useful for resisting a naive “transparency fix.” Even when companies publish high-level descriptions of recommendation inputs—as Hern notes TikTok has done in broad strokes—the *weighting*, *interaction effects*, and *experimental interventions* remain difficult for outsiders to reconstruct (Hern). In that gap, users construct folk theories; regulators demand disclosures; journalists infer business incentives. Zuboff’s vocabulary helps name what is at stake in the gap itself: it is a zone where interpretive authority remains concentrated on the custodial side, even as the user is asked to behave as if the platform were a neutral mirror.

Taken together, these sources support a thesis that short-form video platforms should be read as *rendering engines* embedded in broader political economies of data. The user’s phenomenological experience is “content,” but the platform’s operational experience of the user is *behavioral inventory*.

## Why Short-Form Video Intensifies Surplus-Friendly Traces

Short-form video feeds are not unique in producing behavioral data, but they are unusually efficient at producing *dense micro-behavior* at scale. The interface rewards rapid iteration: many videos per minute, frequent implicit choices (watch, skip, rewatch), and intermittent explicit signals (likes, shares, comments, follows). From a machine-learning perspective, such environments provide high-dimensional time series with frequent labels and pseudo-labels. From a Zuboffian perspective, the same environment produces a *thick surplus stream*: many small actions that can be aggregated into durable models of attention, taste, and vulnerability.

Taina Bucher’s work on algorithmic power helps explain why users experience platforms as both magical and coercive. Algorithmic systems do not only filter content; they shape what users imagine is possible and what they believe “the system” knows (Bucher 18–24). On short-form feeds, folk theories about “the algorithm” circulate widely—sometimes as empowerment (“it gets me”), sometimes as anxiety (“it trapped me”)—precisely because the causal chain between micro-behavior and future content is partially obscured. That obscurity is not an accidental UX flaw; it is compatible with a political economy in which interpretive power remains asymmetric.

Industry-facing and popular coverage of recommendation systems likewise suggests—without needing to claim access to proprietary code—that engagement optimization is central to product design. Journalistic accounts frequently describe feedback loops in which signals of attention train models that serve more of what retains users, raising normative questions about autonomy and manipulation even when legal compliance is asserted (Hern). Alex Hern’s reporting in *The Guardian* crystallizes a widely repeated industry narrative: the For You feed learns quickly from interaction patterns and experimentation, and short-form pacing increases how much behavioral signal a session can generate compared with slower media (Hern). Such journalism is a secondary source not because it proves a specific engineering implementation, but because it documents a publicly intelligible *business model* aligned with surplus extraction: more behavioral traces, tighter tuning, higher retention.

Poell, Nieborg, and van Dijck’s synthesis of platform logics further supports the claim that cultural production has been reorganized around *datafied* audience relationships (Poell et al. 1–10). Short-form video is an exemplar of that reorganization: creators and viewers are linked through metrics, and content success is inseparable from measurable engagement. In Zuboff’s terms, the “product” is not only videos; it is also the continuous refinement of predictive models of audience behavior.

At this point, a reader might object that users *want* engaging feeds, and that optimizing for engagement is simply good design. The reply is not puritanical rejection of pleasure; it is structural. When engagement becomes the central optimization target, platforms inherit incentives to treat attention as a mineable resource. Dense micro-signals make the mine productive. Hern’s reporting underscores the experiential edge case that makes the political economy vivid: users describe feeds becoming “uncannily” attuned to their interests, while the underlying system remains opaque and unevenly accountable (Hern). That combination—felt intimacy plus institutional opacity—is where Zuboff’s rendering metaphor bites hardest.

## Data Portability as Partial Counter-Rendering

If surveillance capitalism thrives when rendering is invisible, then interventions that increase legibility—even imperfectly—have political and pedagogical value. One such intervention is *data portability*: the ability to request an export of information associated with an account. Portability does not grant outsiders access to a platform’s secret ranking function. It can, however, reveal *categories of operationalized life* the platform admits to retaining: activity histories, search logs, social interactions, device and login metadata, and more, depending on jurisdiction and request options.

This is where the idea of *counter-rendering* becomes useful as a design metaphor rather than a technical specification. Counter-rendering names an effort to reconstruct, from disclosed traces, a user-facing map of how lived interaction could be translated into behavioral inventory. Such a map will always be incomplete. Exports omit fields; timestamps may be coarse; some signals never appear because they are inferred on the server but not returned to the user. Nonetheless, the exercise can show *that translation occurs* and can highlight *which kinds of traces* are especially surplus-heavy (for example, dense chronicles of attention and search language).

The interpretive discipline matters. A responsible counter-rendering tool should present outputs as *heuristic approximations* of inference pathways, not as verified descriptions of internal models—precisely because portability is not transparency in the strong sense. Gillespie’s caution about platform black boxes applies: users see outputs and occasional disclosures, not the full custodial machinery (Gillespie 190–95). Couldry and Mejias likewise remind us that data relations are colonial in effect even when they are packaged as consumer choice (Couldry and Mejias 60–65). Portability can mitigate ignorance without dissolving power asymmetry.

Portability also arrives unevenly across jurisdictions and product surfaces. A user may receive JSON, TXT, or nested folders; some categories may be missing; retention windows may exclude the most recent days; and the export may reference files internal to the archive rather than inlining all activity. These limitations do not invalidate portability as a pedagogical resource, but they do define its epistemic ceiling. Counter-rendering, if it is to be intellectually honest, must treat the export as *partial evidence* of operational categories, not as a complete autobiography of surveillance.

### A design imagination: counter-rendering as interface

None of the scholarly sources cited here prescribe a specific user interface, but their arguments converge on a pedagogical need: make translation visible. A counter-rendering interface can operationalize that need in modest ways. For example, it can list discrete “events” reconstructed from the export, then annotate each with (a) a plain-language account of how such a trace might be *rendered* into behavioral inventory, and (b) a cautious judgment about whether the trace resembles *behavioral surplus* in Zuboff’s sense—understood heuristically, not legally. It can additionally compute *temporal density*: bursts of many timestamped events in short clock windows are especially “surplus-friendly,” because they give models rapid feedback about preferences and vulnerabilities. Such features do not decode proprietary models; they stage an argument about *how extraction can work* using the user’s own disclosed artifacts.

This design imagination matters methodologically for a course paper because it connects theory to praxis without confusing the two. The interface is an *interpretive scaffold*, not a forensic instrument. Its legitimacy comes from disciplined rhetoric: it should visibly distinguish evidence (what appears in the export) from inference (what platforms *might* do with similar signals). In that sense, counter-rendering is closer to legal-realist or journalistic sensemaking than to reverse engineering.

Recent empirical work using data donations reinforces this methodological stance. Zannettou et al. analyze real-user TikTok traces at scale and evaluate recommendation effectiveness through engagement proxies (time spent, attention/watch-through, likes), rather than claiming direct access to internal ranking weights (Zannettou et al.). Their findings are especially useful for this project’s scope: engagement can increase over time while attention completion remains relatively stable, indicating that “more engagement” does not reduce to a single metric and should be interpreted multidimensionally. For a learning-oriented counter-rendering tool, this supports an evidence model that reports distinct measurable constructs (consumption volume, attention proxies, interaction signals) instead of one sensationalized “algorithm score.”

This complements earlier user-centric audit work by Boeker and Urman, who show through controlled sock-puppet experiments that factors such as language, location, follow, like, and viewing duration can measurably shift recommendations, while also emphasizing temporal instability and black-box limits (Boeker and Urman). Taken together, these studies justify a conservative mixed evidence strategy: use real-user exports/data donations for user-facing interpretation, and use controlled audits to test sensitivity of candidate factors over time.

The same paper also helps clarify data strategy. Real-user donation/export traces are better suited for individual-level interpretation than synthetic-only bot traces, while still requiring caution about missing fields and black-box internals (Zannettou et al.). By contrast, third-party scraped hashtag datasets (e.g., public Kaggle dumps of popular hashtag posts) are best treated as *context datasets*: useful for mapping topic ecosystems, testing parsers, and stress-testing feature extraction coverage, but not valid for direct claims about what any specific user’s feed has inferred. Keeping these datasets analytically separate protects both rigor and trust.

## Mitigation Without Magical Thinking

If the paper ended at portability, it would risk implying that individual downloads solve structural problems. They do not. Still, mitigation is not meaningless. Zuboff’s analysis points toward collective and regulatory remedies; at the individual level, the honest aim is *harm reduction* and *interrupting feedback loops one can influence*: changing interaction rhythms, reducing surplus density, tightening privacy settings through official menus, segmenting contexts of use, and avoiding the fantasy that “data detox” apps can reverse rendering entirely.

Here Bucher’s insights about algorithmic imaginaries are again relevant: users often want a story in which they can “beat” the algorithm. A deflationary, evidence-based story is less satisfying but more accurate: partial visibility, partial agency, and persistent asymmetry (Bucher 40–45). Counter-rendering aligns with that deflationary stance if it treats exports as *evidence of operational categories*, not as a complete autobiography of surveillance.

Mitigation proposals should also avoid moralizing individual users as if scroll habits were purely private failures. Zuboff’s analysis is institutional: surveillance capitalism is a regime of accumulation, not a personal weakness epidemic (Zuboff 8–11). Still, institutional critique does not require pretending individuals have zero maneuver room. Users can sometimes reduce surplus *density*—the rate at which they supply legible micro-signals—by changing rhythms of use, reducing search-as-discovery inside a commercial feed, tightening official privacy settings, and avoiding risky third-party data practices that claim to “expose” algorithms without consent frameworks. The point is proportionality: mitigation is harm reduction inside an asymmetric system, not an exit from politics.

## Conclusion

Zuboff’s concepts of rendering and behavioral surplus offer a precise vocabulary for describing what short-form video platforms do socially, not only technically: they convert lived attention into data products that serve optimization regimes elsewhere. Platform scholarship clarifies that this process is also custodial and cultural: ranking and visibility are governance. Short-form feeds intensify the generation of surplus-friendly traces because they industrialize micro-behavior at scale. Official data exports cannot reverse that asymmetry, but they can support a limited practice of counter-rendering that makes the conversion from experience to operational facts less mystifying—especially when paired with careful epistemic humility about what remains hidden.

The broader lesson for media and democracy courses is not “delete every app,” but “map the render.” Surveillance capitalism is not defeated by awareness alone, yet ignorance reliably benefits extractive systems. A pedagogical practice of reading one’s own export—slowly, collectively, and without sensationalism—can be a small step toward reclaiming interpretive agency in an era organized around behavioral prediction.

One final limitation deserves emphasis. This paper’s argument is strongest where it is most modest: short-form platforms plausibly intensify surplus-friendly traces; portability plausibly improves legibility; neither claim entails access to secret weights. If future coursework or empirical projects extend this line of inquiry, the appropriate next step is mixed methods: combine textual analysis of disclosed policies and exports with careful qualitative work on user sensemaking (along lines suggested by Bucher), while refusing the fantasy of total technical transparency.

## Data Description

### Source

Personal TikTok data export (`user_data_tiktok.json`, 18.5 MB) requested via **Profile → Settings and privacy → Account → Download your data** and downloaded as a ZIP archive. The export covers account activity from **2019-01-13 to 2026-04-22** (~7.3 years).

### Unit of Observation

One row = **one session**. A session is a contiguous block of user activity events separated from adjacent events by gaps of fewer than 30 minutes (the standard inactivity threshold used across the analysis codebase). Sessions are derived from raw events; they are not a native export concept.

### Label Definition

`binge = 1` if the session falls on a calendar day whose total event count is (a) in the user's personal top 10th percentile of daily volumes **and** (b) at least 2× the user's median daily event count. Both conditions must hold; this anchors the label to the individual's own baseline rather than a population norm. Threshold: ≥ 36 events/day (90th percentile = 36, median = 9).

### Limitations

- **Export completeness:** TikTok returns only the data it discloses under applicable regulations. Watch history is capped at 60,541 entries (likely a platform-side truncation); internal ranking signals, server-side inferences, and A/B experiment assignments are not included.
- **Single user:** The dataset represents one account's activity. All thresholds (top-10%, 2× median) are person-relative; results should not be generalized to a population without replication across multiple exports.
- **No watch-duration signal:** The export records that a video was opened but not how long it was watched. The `attention` primitive is binary (opened vs. not), which underrepresents passive scrolling depth.
- **Timestamp coarseness:** All timestamps are second-precision UTC. Session boundaries at exactly 30 minutes are a modeling choice, not a ground truth behavioral segmentation.

## Data Preprocessing

Full pipeline is in `preprocessing.ipynb` / `preprocessing_executed.ipynb`. Each decision is documented below.

### Missing-Value Handling

Zero null values exist in any column. This is by construction: every session row derives from at least one timestamped event, so temporal features (`hour_of_day`, `day_of_week`) are always defined; share features are fractions over a known denominator; and `peak_epm` is floored at 1 minute to prevent division-by-zero.

High zero-rates on several features reflect **genuine sparsity**, not missing data:

| Feature | Zero rate | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| `search_share` | 97% | Most sessions contain no searches |
| `cascade_count` | 88% | Search→watch chaining is rare |
| `watch_share` | 72% | Many sessions are likes/favorites only |

These zeros carry signal (distinguishing passive preference sessions from active watch sessions) and are retained as-is.

### Categorical Encoding

Two integer features are cyclically encoded because time is circular — treating hour 23 and hour 0 as maximally distant (as raw integers do) is incorrect.

**Method: cyclical encoding** via sine/cosine projection.

| Original feature | Encoding | Output columns | Rationale |
|---|---|---|---|
| `hour_of_day` (0–23) | `sin(x × 2π/24)`, `cos(x × 2π/24)` | `hour_sin`, `hour_cos` | Preserves adjacency of 23→0 |
| `day_of_week` (0–6) | `sin(x × 2π/7)`, `cos(x × 2π/7)` | `dow_sin`, `dow_cos` | Preserves adjacency of Sun→Mon |

Cyclical encoding was preferred over one-hot because it produces 2 columns per feature instead of 24 or 7, avoids the curse of dimensionality, and encodes the correct geometric relationship between time values.

Binary features (`has_search`, `has_social`) required no encoding — they are already 0/1.

### Numeric Scaling

**Method: StandardScaler** (zero mean, unit variance) applied to the 8 continuous features.

MinMaxScaler was rejected because `event_count` is heavily right-skewed (max=1,226, 75th pct=5) — MinMaxScaler would compress 99% of values into the bottom 0.4% of the [0,1] range, effectively zeroing them out for Logistic Regression.

Features excluded from scaling: `has_search`, `has_social` (binary), `hour_sin`, `hour_cos`, `dow_sin`, `dow_cos` (already in [−1, 1]). Tree-based models (Random Forest, GBT) are scale-invariant; scaling is required only for Logistic Regression, but applying it uniformly simplifies the pipeline.

The scaler is fit on **training folds only** inside each CV iteration in `ml_pipeline.py` to prevent data leakage.

### Correlation Findings

From the Pearson correlation heatmap (`plot_correlation_heatmap.png`):

- No feature pair has |r| > 0.80 — no multicollinearity requiring feature removal.
- Strongest positive correlations with the binge label: `event_count`, `peak_epm`, `duration_min` (sessions on binge days are longer and denser).
- `watch_share` has a moderate positive correlation, consistent with binge days being watch-heavy.
- Temporal features (`hour_sin/cos`, `dow_sin/cos`) show near-zero correlation with the target individually, but may contribute to tree-based models through interactions.

### Final Feature Matrix

14 features after preprocessing (12 original → drop 2, add 4 cyclical):

```
Continuous (StandardScaled):  event_count, duration_min, peak_epm,
                               watch_share, search_share, social_share,
                               pref_share, cascade_count
Passthrough (no scaling):     has_search, has_social,
                               hour_sin, hour_cos, dow_sin, dow_cos
```

Preprocessed dataset saved to `sessions_preprocessed.csv` (7,301 rows × 15 columns including label).

## Hyperparameter Tuning

Full pipeline in `tuning.ipynb` / `tuning_executed.ipynb`. All tuning uses `GridSearchCV` with a 3-fold inner CV nested inside 5-fold outer CV. F1 is the selection criterion throughout (preferred over accuracy given 85/15 class imbalance).

### Tuning Grids and Winners

| Model | Parameter(s) | Grid | Best value | Inner CV F1 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Logistic Regression | `C` | [0.001, 0.01, 0.1, 0.5, 1, 10, 100] | **0.001** | 0.8828 |
| Random Forest | `max_depth`, `min_samples_leaf` | [5, 15, None] × [1, 5, 20] | **depth=5, leaf=20** | 0.8870 |
| AdaBoost | `n_estimators`, `learning_rate` | [100, 200, 400] × [0.1, 0.5, 1.0] | **n=400, lr=1.0** | 0.8897 |

### Interpretation of Best Parameters

**Logistic Regression `C=0.001`:** Strong regularisation is preferred, meaning the model benefits from being pulled toward small coefficients. This is consistent with features like `event_count` being heavily right-skewed — without regularisation, the coefficient for this feature would dominate the decision boundary.

**Random Forest `max_depth=5, min_samples_leaf=20`:** Shallow trees with large leaves win over fully-grown trees. The dataset has 7,301 sessions with 14 features — deep trees can memorise individual binge-day sessions, so aggressive pruning via `min_samples_leaf=20` is the right call.

**AdaBoost `n_estimators=400, learning_rate=1.0`:** More stumps at full step size. Because each stump is a max-depth-1 tree (very weak), 400 rounds don't overfit and the full learning rate makes each round maximally informative.
